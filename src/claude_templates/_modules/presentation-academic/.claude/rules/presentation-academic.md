# Academic Presentation — Best Practices

## General Principles

Academic presentations communicate research findings to scholarly audiences. The goal is clarity, rigor, and engagement — not flash. Every slide must respect the audience's expertise while making complex material accessible.

## Presentation Structure

| Section | Slides | Purpose |
|---------|--------|---------|
| Title | 1 | Title, authors, affiliations, conference/date |
| Motivation | 1-2 | Why this problem matters, gap in literature |
| Research question | 1 | Crisp statement of what you investigate |
| Literature positioning | 1-2 | Where you sit relative to prior work (not a full lit review) |
| Method / Data | 2-3 | Design, identification strategy, data sources |
| Results | 3-5 | Main findings with tables/figures, one per slide |
| Robustness / Extensions | 1-2 | Sensitivity checks, heterogeneity |
| Contribution & Implications | 1-2 | What we learn, why it matters, policy or future work |

## Slide Design for Academia

- **Title = finding, not topic.** "Trade openness increases growth by 1.2pp" not "Results."
- **One table or figure per slide.** Never stack multiple results on one slide.
- **Highlight the key coefficient.** Use color or bold to draw the eye to your main result in a regression table.
- **Minimize text, maximize visuals.** Audiences read tables; they listen to you for context.
- **Time allocation**: Plan ~1 minute per slide. A 20-minute talk = ~18 content slides + 2 buffer.

## Tables and Figures

- Tables: show only the key variables. Move full tables to appendix.
- Highlight the coefficient of interest in bold or color.
- Report standard errors in parentheses, significance stars with footnote defining thresholds.
- Figures: label axes with variable names and units, include confidence intervals.
- Every table/figure has a descriptive title that states the finding.
- Source line: dataset name, sample size, time period.

## Speaker Notes

- Write full talking points in speaker notes, not on slides.
- Include transition phrases: "This motivates our next question..." to maintain flow.
- Note likely audience questions per slide and prepare responses.
- Time stamps: note cumulative minutes at key slides to stay on track.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Spending too long on literature review — you need 1-2 slides, not 5.
- Showing every robustness check — pick 2-3 key ones, put rest in appendix.
- Reading the slide aloud — the audience can read; add value with interpretation.
- Tiny fonts in regression tables — if they can't read it from row 10, enlarge or simplify.
- No "roadmap" slide listing "1. Introduction, 2. Literature, 3. Data..." — the agenda is obvious from the talk flow.

## Appendix Strategy

Prepare 5-10 appendix slides for Q&A:
- Full regression tables (all controls shown)
- Alternative specifications
- Data construction details
- Variable definitions
- Robustness checks not shown in main body

## Self-Review — Academic Specific

After building the deck, verify:

- [ ] Research question is stated in one sentence on one slide
- [ ] Identification strategy is clearly explained (not just named)
- [ ] Key result is visually highlighted in the results table
- [ ] Every table/figure has a finding-based title
- [ ] Slide count matches time slot (1 min/slide rule)
- [ ] Appendix covers likely Q&A topics
- [ ] No slide has more text than the presenter will discuss
- [ ] Contribution is stated explicitly, not left for the audience to infer
