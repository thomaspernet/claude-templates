# Economics Research Paper — Best Practices

## Paper Structure (Mandatory)

Every empirical economics paper follows this structure. Do not deviate without explicit justification.

1. **Title page** — title, author, abstract (100-150 words), keywords, JEL codes, acknowledgments
2. **Introduction** — the most important section; must answer WHAT, WHY, and HOW
3. **Related literature** — max 3 strands; complement, never criticize
4. **Data** — one subsection per variable set; include descriptive statistics
5. **Empirical strategy** — convince the reader your identification strategy is valid
6. **Results** — self-explanatory tables; discuss economic magnitude, not just significance
7. **Robustness checks** — alternative stories, alternative measures, outlier sensitivity
8. **Conclusion** — max 3 pages; contribution, policy implications, future research
9. **References** — alphabetical, consistent style, complete

## Introduction Framework

The introduction must address these questions, in roughly this order:

| Question | What it answers |
|----------|----------------|
| **WHAT 1** | What is your research question? |
| **WHY 1** | Why is the answer consequential? |
| **WHY 2** | Why isn't the answer obvious? (endogeneity, competing theories) |
| **WHAT 2** | What is your contribution to the literature? |
| **HOW 1** | What data do you use? |
| **HOW 2** | What is your empirical strategy? (and why it solves endogeneity) |
| **WHAT 3** | What are your findings? |

Start with a real-world contextualization paragraph (statistics, examples, anecdotes). End with a roadmap. Upper limit: 5 pages.

## Writing Rules

1. **Short, meaningful sentences.** Every sentence must have a subject, verb, and object.
2. **Reproducibility.** A fellow graduate student should be able to reproduce every number from the information in the paper — never be allusive.
3. **Brevity.** Continually ask: "Can I express the same idea in less space?" Paper should not exceed 40 pages (tables and figures included).
4. **Simplicity.** Less math is better. Simpler estimation techniques are better.
5. **Active tense.** "I estimate equation (1)" not "Equation (1) is estimated."
6. **No vague intensifiers.** Avoid "very", "extremely", "highly."
7. **Precise referencing.** Never start a sentence with "This" unless followed by the associated noun: "This evidence indicates" not "This indicates."
8. **Paragraph discipline.** One idea per paragraph. No paragraph longer than half a page.
9. **Footnotes.** If the reader should stop and read it, put it in the text. If not, delete it. Long reference lists or simple algebra are acceptable footnotes.

## Tables and Figures

- Tables must be **self-explanatory** — a reader should understand a table without the text, and vice versa.
- Always discuss **economic magnitude**, not just statistical significance. In large datasets, even tiny effects are "statistically significant."
- Use scatterplots to document correlations (e.g., instrument vs. endogenous variable).

## Literature Review Rules

- Decompose into no more than 3 strands.
- Only cite papers closely linked to your research question.
- Use your own words — never give the impression of copied abstracts.
- Frame contributions positively: "We complement these approaches" not "The deficiency of X's approach."

## Research Question Quality Test

Before committing to a research question, it must pass two tests:

1. **"Why isn't the answer obvious?"** — You should not be hoping for a specific result. Multiple outcomes must be plausible.
2. **"Why should I care?"** — Even non-experts should feel the conclusions matter for real-world well-being.

If you cannot answer both convincingly, the question is not ready.
