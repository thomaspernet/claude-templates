# Finance Presentation — Best Practices

## General Principles

Finance presentations must convey credibility, precision, and structured thinking. Whether pitching to investors, presenting to a board, or delivering an earnings readout, the audience evaluates both your numbers and your ability to think about those numbers clearly. Every claim must be grounded in data, every projection must state its assumptions, and every recommendation must quantify the expected impact.

## Presentation Types

| Type | Audience | Key Focus |
|------|----------|-----------|
| Investor / Pitch deck | VCs, angels, PE | Growth story, market size, unit economics, ask |
| Board presentation | Board of directors | Performance vs. plan, strategic decisions, risks |
| Earnings / Quarterly review | Analysts, investors | Actuals vs. guidance, segment performance, outlook |
| Investment memo | Investment committee | Thesis, risks, returns, deal structure |
| Financial planning | Leadership team | Budget, forecasts, scenario analysis |

## Deck Structure — Investor / Pitch Deck

| Section | Slides | Purpose |
|---------|--------|---------|
| Cover | 1 | Company, tagline, date |
| Problem | 1 | Pain point you solve (customer quote or stat) |
| Solution | 1-2 | Your product/service, how it works |
| Market | 1 | TAM → SAM → SOM with sources |
| Traction | 1-2 | Revenue, growth rate, key milestones |
| Business model | 1 | How you make money, unit economics |
| Financials | 1-2 | Revenue projections (3-5yr), key assumptions, path to profitability |
| Team | 1 | Founders + key hires, relevant experience |
| Ask | 1 | Raise amount, use of funds, milestones it unlocks |
| Appendix | 3-5 | Detailed financials, competitive landscape, technical details |

## Financial Data Presentation

### Numbers and Metrics

- **Round appropriately.** $1.2M not $1,234,567. Precision signals differ by context: earnings reports need exact figures; pitch decks need round numbers.
- **Show trends, not just snapshots.** Always include at least 3 time periods for comparison.
- **Benchmark everything.** A number in isolation means nothing. Compare to: prior period, plan/budget, industry average, competitor.
- **Use consistent units.** Pick $K, $M, or $B and stick with it throughout.
- **State assumptions explicitly.** Every projection slide must list the 2-3 key assumptions driving the numbers.

### Financial Charts

| Metric Type | Chart | Details |
|-------------|-------|---------|
| Revenue over time | Vertical bar or line | Show growth rate as annotation |
| Revenue breakdown | Stacked bar or waterfall | By segment, product, or geography |
| Margins and ratios | Line chart with benchmark line | Gross margin, EBITDA margin trends |
| Unit economics | Table or structured text | CAC, LTV, payback period, LTV/CAC |
| Projections | Bar chart (actuals) + line (forecast) | Clearly mark where actuals end and projections begin |
| Scenarios | Table with 3 columns | Bear / Base / Bull with key assumptions per row |

### Table Design for Finance

- Right-align all numbers
- Use consistent decimal places (1 decimal for %, 0 for whole $M)
- Bold totals and subtotals
- Shade alternating rows for readability
- Mark actuals vs. projections with different formatting (e.g., projections in italic)
- Include growth rates as a separate row (YoY %, QoQ %)

## Credibility Signals

- Source every external data point (market size, industry benchmarks)
- Show actuals alongside projections to demonstrate track record
- Acknowledge risks explicitly — a risk slide signals maturity, not weakness
- Use scenario analysis (bear/base/bull) instead of single-point forecasts
- Show bottoms-up unit economics, not just top-down TAM math

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- Hockey-stick projections with no justification
- TAM math that assumes unrealistic market capture
- Missing assumptions on financial projections
- Mixing up revenue, gross profit, and EBITDA in comparisons
- Too many metrics on one slide — pick 3-4 KPIs, not 15
- Showing only best-case scenario
- No competitive positioning — investors know competitors exist
- Tiny numbers in dense tables that no one can read

## Self-Review — Finance Specific

After building the deck, verify:

- [ ] Every projection slide states its key assumptions
- [ ] Numbers are benchmarked (vs. prior period, plan, or industry)
- [ ] Units are consistent throughout ($K/$M/$B, consistent decimals)
- [ ] Actuals and projections are clearly distinguished
- [ ] Growth rates shown alongside absolute numbers
- [ ] Market sizing is bottoms-up, not just top-down
- [ ] Risks are acknowledged, not hidden
- [ ] Ask is specific: amount, use of funds, milestones
- [ ] No hockey-stick without supporting evidence
- [ ] Financial tables are readable from a distance (18pt+ fonts)
