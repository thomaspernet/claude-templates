# Conducting a Peer Review of an Economics Paper

> Guidelines for producing structured, actionable peer reviews for top-tier journals (AER, QJE, JDE, JIE, RESTUD).

## Purpose

A peer review serves three audiences:

1. **The editor** — needs a clear recommendation and a concise summary of strengths and weaknesses to make a decision
2. **The authors** — need specific, actionable feedback to improve the paper
3. **The field** — benefits when the review raises the quality bar without gatekeeping legitimate contributions

A good review is honest, constructive, and precise. It identifies problems and, wherever possible, suggests how to fix them.

## Review Structure

### 1. Summary (1 paragraph)

- Restate the paper's question, method, main finding, and claimed contribution in your own words
- This signals to the editor and authors that you understood the paper
- Do not evaluate here — just describe
- If you cannot summarize the paper in one paragraph, that itself is a finding about the paper's clarity

### 2. Overall Assessment (1-2 paragraphs)

- State your overall judgment: is the contribution sufficient for the target journal?
- Identify the paper's main strength (novel data, clean identification, important question, new mechanism)
- Identify the paper's main weakness (identification concern, limited external validity, incomplete analysis)
- Be direct: "The paper is publishable in JDE conditional on addressing the identification issues in Section 4" is more useful than "The paper has some interesting results but also some concerns"

### 3. Major Issues (3-5 max)

Problems that, if unresolved, prevent publication. Each must include:

- **What** the problem is, stated precisely
- **Why** it matters for the paper's conclusions
- **How** to fix it, if you have a suggestion

| Category | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| Identification | Endogeneity, parallel trends violation, SUTVA concerns, bad instruments |
| Sample/data | Selection bias, survivorship bias, measurement error, inadequate N |
| Specification | Wrong estimator, omitted variable bias, post-treatment controls |
| Interpretation | Results don't support claims, alternative explanations not ruled out, overstated external validity |
| Missing analysis | No robustness, no heterogeneity, mechanisms asserted not tested |

### 4. Minor Issues

Problems that should be fixed but don't threaten the core contribution:

- Unclear exposition in specific paragraphs
- Missing variable definitions
- Table formatting issues
- Citations to add or correct
- Inconsistencies between text and tables

### 5. Detailed Comments (optional but valuable)

Page-by-page or section-by-section comments, formatted as:
- **p.X / Section Y / Table Z**: specific comment

### 6. Recommendation

- **Accept** (rare on first submission)
- **Revise and resubmit (major)**: contribution is there, significant work needed
- **Revise and resubmit (minor)**: paper is close, fix specific issues
- **Reject**: fundamental problems that cannot be addressed through revision

## Review Timeline

| Phase | Time | What to Do |
|-------|------|-----------|
| First read | 1-2h | Cover to cover, no notes. Form general impression. Can you state question, finding, contribution? |
| Second read | 2-3h | Read with a pen. Mark issues, check numbers, note identification concerns |
| Writing | 1-2h | Organize into structure above |
| Cooling period | 1 day | Re-read before submitting to check tone and proportionality |

Total: 5-8 hours spread over 2-3 days. If the paper requires significantly more time, that may indicate it needs revision for clarity.
