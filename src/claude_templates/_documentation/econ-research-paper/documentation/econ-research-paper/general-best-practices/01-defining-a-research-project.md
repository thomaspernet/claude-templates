# Defining a Good Economics Research Project

> Source: Valfort (2014), "Research Methodology 1: Defining a good research project", Pantheon-Sorbonne University.

## What Makes a Good Research Project

A good research project must be based on an interesting research question that leads to a **new** and **feasible** contribution.

An interesting research question is:
- A question you feel genuine passion for (you'll spend months or years on it)
- A question whose answer is **not obvious**
- A question whose answer is **consequential** for real-world well-being

## The Two Skeptic Tests

For every research question, imagine the most skeptical person pressing you with:

### Test 1: "Why isn't the answer obvious?"

- If estimating the impact of A on B:
  - Identify endogeneity problems (reverse causality, omitted variables, selection bias) that could explain why this impact might not exist
  - Identify why the magnitude could be strongly biased under naive OLS
  - Identify circumstances that could make the impact heterogeneous across populations
- If testing a theory:
  - Present competing theories that predict both a negative and positive impact

**Key principle:** You should never find yourself hoping for a specific result. If you describe some results as "good" and others as "bad," the question isn't truly interesting.

### Test 2: "Why should I care?"

- For each possible answer, ask: "What policy recommendations could this lead to?"
- Even a non-expert should feel that addressing this question leads to conclusions that matter
- A "consequential" answer helps understand microeconomic or macroeconomic mechanisms that affect individuals' well-being

## Finding Sources of Ideas

- Read newspapers, blogs (VoxEU, research digests), watch TED talks with an economics lens
- Regularly visit general-interest journals (AER, QJE) and field journals (JDE, WBER, EDCC)
- Attend seminars — speakers present working papers where more questions are raised than answered
- Maintain a **journal of ideas**: whenever you have a research idea, write it down immediately
- React to statements by economists, politicians, or journalists that puzzle you or make you angry — passion fuels research

## Defining a New Contribution

"New" does not mean revolutionizing a field. It means extending understanding of some mechanism in a way that leads to more fine-tuned policy recommendations.

After reading your paper, researchers should see the world differently because you have:
- Identified a clear causal (and perhaps unsuspected) relationship between variables
- Cast doubt on established views by showing data doesn't support prevailing theory

### How to Find Your Contribution

1. **Literature search**: Use keywords to find closely related published papers. Start with review journals (Annual Review of Economics, Journal of Economic Literature, JEP, JES).
2. **Find the frontier**: Published papers reflect thinking from years ago. Check Google Scholar, NBER working papers, researcher websites for the latest work.
3. **Read critically**: For each paper closely related to your question, ask:
   - Can this empirical strategy be applied to other countries/periods?
   - How could the identification strategy be improved? (Better instruments? More compelling evaluation technique?)
   - What else could be done with this dataset? (Check codebooks)
   - Are there unexploited implications?
4. **Dying issues**: If the most recent papers on a topic are 5+ years old, the question may be dying. Think twice before proceeding.
5. **Substantial, not incremental**: Adding one control variable to someone else's regression is not a thesis. The contribution must be theoretically motivated and substantial.

## Feasibility

Feasibility means availability of data that allow you to implement an identification strategy that provides an answer **purged of endogeneity bias**.

When evaluating feasibility:
- Search for the optimal dataset — one that best addresses your question with a clean identification strategy
- Consider whether data exist that allow you to distinguish between competing stories
- Justify your data source choice when alternatives exist

## The Selection Process

From your journal of ideas, select the research question that is:
1. The **most interesting** (passes both skeptic tests)
2. Leads to the **newest** and most **feasible** contribution

This is your thesis. Invest accordingly — for PhD candidates, this typically becomes the first chapter of the dissertation.

## Practical Habits

- **Create a folder immediately.** Add a running stream of thoughts and suggestions from conversations.
- **Talk to everyone.** Discuss with economists (seminars, advisors) and non-economists (can you explain it clearly?).
- **Be a compulsive note-taker.** Every reaction, suggestion, and potential criticism goes in the folder.
- **Focus intensely.** "Research involves intense concentration over long periods. It is not possible to return casually at irregular intervals." (Creedy, 2001)
- **Be prepared to discard.** Much of what's in your folder won't survive to the final product, but it's critical for convergence.
- **Don't wait to get feedback.** Share early. The risk of saying nothing far outweighs the cost of occasionally saying something unfinished.
