# Writing a Good Economics Research Paper

> Source: Valfort (2014), "Research Methodology 2: Writing a good research paper", Pantheon-Sorbonne University.

## What Makes a Good Research Paper

A good research paper:
- Is based on a good research project
- Is **clearly written**: each sentence is short, meaningful, and logically linked
- Is **reproducible**: a fellow graduate student should be able to reproduce every number from the information in the paper
- Is **short**: continually ask "Can I express the same idea in less space?" (target: under 40 pages including tables)
- Is **simple**: "The less math used, the better. The simpler the estimation technique, the better." (Cochrane, 2005)

## Paper Structure

| Section | Key Requirements |
|---------|-----------------|
| 1. Title page | Title, abstract (100-150 words), keywords, JEL codes, acknowledgments |
| 2. Introduction | Answer WHAT/WHY/HOW — the most critical section |
| 3. Related literature | Max 3 strands, positive framing of contribution |
| 4. Data | One subsection per variable set, descriptive statistics |
| 5. Empirical strategy | Convince reader identification strategy is valid |
| 6. Results | Self-explanatory tables, economic magnitude |
| 7. Robustness checks | Alternative stories, alternative measures |
| 8. Conclusion | Max 3 pages: contribution, policy, future research |
| 9. References | Alphabetical, consistent, complete |

## Section-by-Section Guide

### 1. Title Page

**Title:** Present the research question in an eye-catching way. Examples of effective titles:
- "Good bye Lenin (or not?): the effect of communism on people's preferences"
- "The slave trade and the origin of mistrust in Africa"
- "Why do some countries produce so much more output per worker than others?"

**Abstract:** 100-150 words. Communicate the main result so readers will want to continue. Write it **last** — after the conclusion and introduction.

**Keywords:** No more than 2 lines.

**JEL codes:** Use the AEA classification system. Include codes for methodology (e.g., C36 for IV) and field (e.g., I21 for education). These facilitate discovery via Econlit and search engines.

### 2. Introduction

The introduction is the most important section. "If an apple does not taste good at the first bite, one simply throws it away" (Kwan Choi, 2002).

It must answer three sets of questions:

**WHAT questions:**
1. What is your research question? (WHAT 1)
2. What is new / what is your contribution? (WHAT 2)
3. What are your findings? (WHAT 3)

**WHY questions:**
1. Why is the answer consequential? (WHY 1)
2. Why isn't the answer obvious? (WHY 2)

**HOW questions:**
1. How do you proceed regarding data? (HOW 1)
2. How do you proceed regarding empirical strategy? (HOW 2)

**Structure:**
1. Contextualization paragraph — ground in real world (statistics, examples, anecdotes). Max 1 paragraph.
2. WHAT 1 — objective of the paper
3. WHY 1 — why the answer is consequential
4. WHY 2 — why the answer is not obvious (endogeneity, competing theories, heterogeneity)
5. WHAT 2 — contribution to the literature
6. HOW 1 — data presentation
7. HOW 2 — empirical strategy (explain why it solves endogeneity)
8. WHAT 3 — results (discuss magnitude, not just significance)
9. Roadmap — last paragraph

**Rules:**
- Upper limit: 5 pages
- Crystal-clear: a non-specialist or newcomer should understand every point
- After the introduction, deliver main results as fast as possible: "There should be nothing before the main result that a reader does not need to know in order to understand the main result" (Cochrane, 2005)

### 3. Related Literature

- Summarize in no more than 3 strands
- Only cite papers closely linked to your question
- Use your own clear words — never copy-paste abstracts
- Frame contributions positively: "We complement these approaches in a number of ways. First... Second... Third..."
- Never use: "the deficiency of X's approach", "the problem of these papers", "these papers suffer from"
- You may use this section to elaborate on WHY 2 (why the answer is not obvious)

### 4. Data

- Present data for each set of variables in separate subsections: dependent variable, explanatory variable, instrument (if applicable), controls
- When multiple data sources exist for a variable, justify your choice
- Include descriptive statistics: N, mean, SD, min, max
- Comment on the descriptive statistics — don't just present the table

### 5. Empirical Strategy

- Describe the strategy in detail
- Convince the reader it solves endogeneity
- For IV: explain why the instrument affects Y only through X (exclusion restriction)
- Use visual arguments: scatterplots of bivariate relationships (e.g., instrument vs. endogenous variable)

### 6. Results

- **Write regression equations explicitly.** Define every variable and explain what each coefficient captures.
- **Tables must be self-explanatory.** "A reader should understand a table without reference to the text, and the text should stand by itself even if a reader ignores the tables" (Levine).
- **Focus on economic magnitude.** "Especially in large panel data sets even the tiniest of effects is 'statistically significant'" (Cochrane, 2005). Put magnitudes in perspective.

### 7. Robustness Checks

First priority — alternative stories:
- Identify additional channels through which your instrument could affect the dependent variable
- Show findings survive when controlling for these channels

Second priority — sensitivity tests:
- Alternative measures of dependent variable
- Alternative measures of explanatory variables
- Alternative instruments
- Outlier deletion

### 8. Conclusion

Max 3 pages:
1. Summarize your contribution
2. Emphasize policy implications
3. Point to avenues for future research (acknowledge limitations without excessive negativity)

### 9. References

- Spell out all author names (no abbreviations like "MSS")
- Indicate year of publication in parentheses
- Alphabetical order, consistent style for capitalization, italics, and ordering

## Writing Tips

### Sentence-Level

- Every sentence: subject, verb, object
- Use "I" for sole-authored papers, but vary with "This paper contributes to..." or "Table 4 presents..." to avoid repetition
- Active tense: "I estimate equation (1)" not "Equation (1) is estimated"
- No vague intensifiers: avoid "very", "extremely", "highly"
- Never start with bare "This" — always follow with the noun: "This evidence indicates" not "This indicates"

### Paragraph-Level

- Each paragraph contains **one** idea
- No paragraph longer than half a page
- Find ways to break up long paragraphs even if they contain a single idea — readers look for open space

### Footnotes

Follow Cochrane's test:
- "Do you really want the reader to stop and read this? Then it should be in the text."
- "Do you think the average reader should not stop? Then delete the footnote."
- Acceptable footnotes: long reference lists, simple algebra, documentation details

### The Writing Process

1. **Sketch** the content of each section briefly
2. **Generate** the text section by section
3. **Re-read as you go**: before a new paragraph, re-read the previous one; before a new session, re-read previous work (improves continuity)
4. **Revise multiple times**: "All drafts must be edited and polished many times" (Creedy, 2001)
5. **Be your own critic**: put yourself in the reader's position, anticipate objections
6. **Let it rest**: leave the paper alone for a while, then return refreshed for a final polish

### Getting Inspiration

Study papers by economists known for clear writing (use their papers as stylistic templates):
- Daron Acemoglu, Nathan Nunn, Andrei Shleifer, and others in your field
- "Getting inspiration" means imitating clear phrases and structures, then adapting them to your research — this is about style, not ideas
